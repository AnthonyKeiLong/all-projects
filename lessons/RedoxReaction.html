Redox Reactions & Stoichiometry

From Electron Transfer to Quantitative Titration Calculations

1. The Core Concept of Redox

A Redox reaction fundamentally involves the transfer of electrons between chemical species. Because electrons cannot exist on their own in a standard chemical system, Oxidation and Reduction must always occur simultaneously.

Remember the universal mnemonic: OIL RIG (Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain of electrons).

Oxidation Half-Equation:
Zn(s) → Zn²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻

Zinc metal loses electrons (is oxidized) and acts as the Reducing Agent.

Reduction Half-Equation:
Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → Cu(s)

Copper(II) ions gain electrons (are reduced) and act as the Oxidizing Agent.

The First Golden Rule of Redox Math

Notice that exactly 2 electrons are transferred per atom in the example above. In macroscopic lab settings, we deal with moles. 1 mole of Zn metal releases 2 moles of electrons. To maintain balance, we require exactly 1 mole of Cu²⁺ to consume those 2 moles of electrons.

This is the unbreakable mathematical rule of redox chemistry: Moles of electrons lost = Moles of electrons gained.

2. Balancing Equations & Titration Calculations

In quantitative chemistry, we use Redox Titrations to find the unknown concentration of a substance. First, we must combine half-equations to find the reacting mole ratio. Let's look at the classic reaction between Permanganate (MnO₄⁻) and Iron(II) (Fe²⁺).

Reduction (Gaining e⁻)
MnO₄⁻ + 8H⁺ + 5e⁻ → Mn²⁺ + 4H₂O

1 mole of MnO₄⁻ requires 5 moles of electrons.

Oxidation (Losing e⁻)
Fe²⁺ → Fe³⁺ + 1e⁻

1 mole of Fe²⁺ provides only 1 mole of electrons.

Finding the Stoichiometric Ratio

To make the electrons balance, we must multiply the Iron half-equation by 5. This tells us the exact mole ratio: 1 mole of MnO₄⁻ reacts with exactly 5 moles of Fe²⁺.

MnO₄⁻ + 5Fe²⁺ + 8H⁺ → Mn²⁺ + 5Fe³⁺ + 4H₂O
Deep Dive: Titration Calculation Example

Let's put the mole ratio to work. Suppose you perform a titration where it takes exactly 20.0 mL of 0.020 M KMnO₄ to completely react with 25.0 mL of an unknown Fe²⁺ solution. What is the concentration of the Fe²⁺ solution?

1
Calculate moles of the known substance (KMnO₄)

Convert volume to Liters (20.0 mL = 0.0200 L) and multiply by Molarity.

Formula: Moles = Concentration (M) × Volume (L)
Math: Moles MnO₄⁻ = 0.020 mol/L × 0.0200 L
= 0.00040 moles MnO₄⁻
2
Use the mole ratio to find moles of the unknown (Fe²⁺)

Multiply the moles of known by the stoichiometric ratio (Unknown / Known).

Ratio: 5 moles Fe²⁺ / 1 mole MnO₄⁻
Math: 0.00040 moles MnO₄⁻ × (5 / 1)
= 0.0020 moles Fe²⁺
3
Calculate the final concentration

Divide the moles of the unknown by its original volume in Liters (25.0 mL = 0.0250 L).

Formula: Concentration = Moles / Volume (L)
Math: [Fe²⁺] = 0.0020 moles / 0.0250 L
= 0.080 M

3. Oxidation Number Math Analyzer

The Algebraic Formula for Oxidation States

To calculate an unknown oxidation state, we use a simple algebraic sum based on fixed rules (e.g., Oxygen is usually -2, Hydrogen is +1, Group 1 metals are +1). The sum of all oxidation states in a molecule or ion must equal its overall charge.

(Atoms of A × Ox State of A) + (Atoms of B × Ox State of B) = Overall Charge
Neutral Molecules (e.g., H₂O):
The overall charge is 0. So, the algebraic sum equals 0.
Polyatomic Ions (e.g., SO₄²⁻):
The overall charge is -2. So, the algebraic sum equals -2.
