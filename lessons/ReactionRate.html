Rate of Reaction

Master the collision theory, understand the factors affecting reaction rates, and learn how to interpret the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution curve for the HKDSE.

Following the Progress of a Reaction

Rate of reaction is the change in concentration (or amount) of a reactant or product per unit time. In the laboratory, we choose a method based on the observable macroscopic changes of the specific reaction.

Volume of Gas Produced

Used when a gas is a product. Measured using a gas syringe or inverted measuring cylinder.

Mg(s) + 2HCl(aq) → MgCl₂(aq) + H₂(g)

Decrease in Mass

Used when a heavy gas (like CO₂) escapes from the system. Measured using an electronic balance.

CaCO₃(s) + 2HCl(aq) → CaCl₂(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)

Colorimetry / Transmittance

Used when a colored species is consumed or formed. A colorimeter measures absorbance.

Br₂(aq) + HCOOH(aq) → 2HBr(aq) + CO₂(g)
Brown → Colorless

Graphical Analysis & Calculations

Interpreting Rate Graphs

In a typical concentration-time or volume-time graph, the curve gets less steep over time as reactants are consumed.

  • 1. Initial Rate (t = 0) The rate at the very beginning. Found by drawing a tangent at t=0 and calculating its slope. This is the maximum rate.
  • 2. Instantaneous Rate The rate at a specific time 't'. Found by drawing a tangent to the curve at that exact time and calculating its slope (Δy / Δx).
  • 3. Average Rate The overall rate over a time interval. Calculated as:
    Total Change in Amount / Total Time Taken

DSE Calculation Walkthrough

Excess CaCO₃(s) reacts with 50.0 cm³ of 2.0 M HCl(aq). The mass of the reaction mixture decreases by 0.88 g in the first 40 seconds.

Q1: Average rate of mass loss (g s⁻¹)?
Rate = Change in mass / Time
Rate = 0.88 g / 40 s
Rate = 0.022 g s⁻¹
Q2: Average rate of CO₂ production (mol s⁻¹)?
Step 1: The mass loss is due to CO₂ escaping. Molar mass of CO₂ = 12.0 + 2(16.0) = 44.0 g mol⁻¹
Moles of CO₂ = 0.88 g / 44.0 g mol⁻¹ = 0.02 mol

Step 2: Calculate rate in moles per second. Rate = 0.02 mol / 40 s
Rate = 0.0005 mol s⁻¹
Q3: What happens to the rate at t = 60s? Explain.
The rate decreases.

Reason: As the reaction proceeds, the concentration of HCl(aq) decreases. The reactant particles are further apart, leading to a lower frequency of effective collisions.

Collision Theory & Factors

The Golden Rules of Collision

For a reaction to occur, reactant particles must collide. However, not all collisions result in a reaction. An effective collision requires two things:

  • Energy ≥ Activation Energy (Ea)

    Particles must collide with kinetic energy greater than or equal to the minimum energy required to break bonds.

  • Correct Orientation

    The reactive parts of the molecules must be aligned perfectly when they strike each other.

HKDSE Keyword Alert: Always use the phrase "frequency of effective collisions" rather than just "more collisions" or "faster collisions" to score the mark.
Concentration / Pressure
Higher concentration means more particles per unit volume. The particles are closer together, leading to a higher frequency of collisions, and thus a higher frequency of effective collisions. Rate increases.
Surface Area (Solid Reactants)
Breaking a solid into a powder exposes more particles to the fluid reactants. This increases the contact area, increasing the frequency of collisions, and thus the frequency of effective collisions. Rate increases.
DSE Question: Catalyst Action

"State the effect of adding a suitable catalyst on the rate of a chemical reaction and explain how the catalyst works. (3 marks)"

1. The rate of reaction increases.
2. A catalyst provides an alternative reaction pathway...
3. ...which has a lower activation energy.
(Bonus connection): Therefore, a larger fraction of particles will have energy ≥ Ea, increasing the frequency of effective collisions.

DSE Marking Scheme: Why does Temperature increase rate?

"When temperature increases, the average kinetic energy of the particles increases. The distribution curve flattens and shifts to the right. A significantly larger fraction of particles now possess kinetic energy greater than or equal to the activation energy (Ea). Thus, the frequency of effective collisions increases, increasing the reaction rate."
