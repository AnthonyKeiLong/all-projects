Nuclear Stability Deep Dive

Stable Isotopes

Isotopes with a balanced ratio of protons to neutrons. The strong nuclear force perfectly balances the electrostatic repulsion between protons.

  • Do not undergo radioactive decay.
  • Make up most of the matter we interact with daily.
Example: Carbon-12 (6p, 6n) and Carbon-13 (6p, 7n) are both entirely stable.
KEY TERM

Radioisotopes

Also known as Radionuclides. These are unstable isotopes of an element caused by having too many or too few neutrons compared to protons.

To achieve stability, the unstable nucleus spontaneously decays, emitting alpha, beta, or gamma radiation.

Example: Carbon-14 (6p, 8n) has too many neutrons and decays via beta emission. It is used in radiocarbon dating.

Unstable Elements

Elements with massive nuclei (Atomic Number > 82). The strong nuclear force has a very short range and cannot overcome the immense electrostatic repulsion of dozens of protons.

No Stable Isotopes: For elements like Uranium (92p) or Plutonium (94p), no amount of neutron "glue" works. Every single isotope of these elements is a radionuclide.

2. Chemical Bonding

Ionic Bonding

Occurs between metals and non-metals. Atoms completely transfer valence electrons to achieve a full, stable outer shell (octet configuration).

  • Metals lose electrons to become positive ions (cations).
  • Non-metals gain electrons to become negative ions (anions).
  • Opposite charges attract via strong electrostatic forces, forming a giant ionic lattice.

Covalent Bonding

Occurs between non-metal atoms. Atoms share pairs of valence electrons because neither atom is willing to completely lose an electron.

  • The shared electrons are strongly attracted to both positive nuclei, holding the atoms together.
  • Can form simple molecules (e.g., H₂O, CO₂) or giant covalent structures (e.g., Diamond, Graphite).

Metallic Bonding

Occurs in pure metals and alloys. Metal atoms pack closely together and their outer valence electrons become "delocalized" (free to move).

  • Creates a regular lattice of positive metal ions immersed in a "sea" of delocalized electrons.
  • The strong electrostatic attraction between the ions and the mobile electrons forms the metallic bond.
  • Explains properties like electrical conductivity and malleability.
