The Question

How long is the coast of the United Kingdom?

It sounds like a straightforward question. You simply take a map, measure the edge, and report the number.

However, if you ask the CIA World Factbook, they will tell you it's roughly 12,429 kilometers. But if you ask the World Resources Institute, they will claim it is 19,717 kilometers.

Who is wrong? Neither of them. They just used differently sized "rulers" to measure it. Welcome to the Coastline Paradox.

The Concept

Fractals and Infinity

In 1967, mathematician Benoit Mandelbrot published a paper explaining this phenomenon. He realized that coastlines are naturally fractal.

Unlike a smooth circle or a straight line, coastlines have inlets, bays, rocks, and pebbles. If you zoom in on a coastline, it looks just as jagged and complex as it did from far away.

Because a coastline infinitely zig-zags on a microscopic level, its theoretical length actually approaches infinity as your measuring tool approaches zero.

The Golden Rule:

"The shorter the ruler you use to measure a coastline, the longer the coastline will appear."

A 100km ruler skips over small bays. A 1km ruler goes into those bays. A 1cm ruler measures around individual boulders.
