The Age of Imperialism

A World Carved by Europe

Over a span of 500 years, a handful of Western European nations established dominion over the vast majority of the globe. Through maritime exploration, military force, economic exploitation, and technological advantage, they built empires of unprecedented scale.

Six distinct powers shaped the modern global map. The pioneers, Spain and Portugal, conquered the Americas in the 1500s. They were later eclipsed by Great Britain, with the largest empire in human history; France, dominating much of Africa and Indochina; the Netherlands, controlling the lucrative East Indies; and finally Belgium, possessing a massive, brutalized territory in Central Africa.

How did these European nations accumulate such immense territories across different centuries? And why did this 500-year global order collapse so rapidly in the 20th century?

The Tipping Points

Centuries of Collapse

Decolonization was not a single event, but occurred in waves across different centuries. The first major wave hit the older empires: Spain and Portugal. Inspired by the American and French Revolutions, Latin America successfully fought for independence in the early 1800s, crippling the Iberian empires.

However, for Britain, France, Belgium, and the Netherlands, their empires only continued to grow, peaking around 1920 after absorbing German and Ottoman territories post-WWI.

The ultimate catalyst was World War II. France, Belgium, and the Netherlands were conquered. Great Britain was bankrupted. The myth of European invincibility was shattered globally, triggering an unstoppable wave of nationalism across Africa and Asia.

The 20th Century Shift

  • 1. Financial Ruin: Maintaining vast global militaries to suppress rebellions became economically impossible for war-torn Europe post-1945.
  • 2. Rise of Superpowers: The USA and USSR emerged as the new global hegemons, both holding strong anti-colonial ideologies.
  • 3. Ideological Shift: Millions of colonial subjects fought to liberate Europe in WWII; returning home, they demanded the same freedom.
