I. Cells & Molecules

1. Molecules of Life

Carbohydrates

  • Monosaccharides: Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆), Fructose - building blocks
  • Disaccharides: Sucrose (glucose + fructose), Lactose (glucose + galactose)
  • Polysaccharides: Starch (energy storage), Glycogen (animal starch), Cellulose (plant cell wall)
  • Test: Benedict's test (blue → brick red for reducing sugars)

Proteins

  • Amino acids: 20 types, linked by peptide bonds
  • Structure: Primary (sequence), Secondary (α-helix/β-pleated), Tertiary (3D shape), Quaternary (multiple polypeptides)
  • Functions: Enzymes, structural (collagen), transport (haemoglobin), hormones
  • Test: Biuret test (blue → purple)

Lipids

  • Triglycerides: Glycerol + 3 fatty acids (saturated/unsaturated)
  • Phospholipids: Hydrophilic head, hydrophobic tails - form cell membranes
  • Functions: Energy storage, insulation, waterproofing
  • Test: Sudan III (red stain), Emulsion test

Nucleic Acids

  • DNA: Deoxyribonucleic acid - double helix, stores genetic info
  • RNA: Ribonucleic acid - single strand, involved in protein synthesis
  • Nucleotides: Phosphate + Sugar + Base (A,T,C,G for DNA; A,U,C,G for RNA)
  • Test: Dische's test (blue color)

3. Movement Across Membranes

Passive Transport

  • Diffusion: Movement of particles from high to low concentration. Factors: Concentration gradient, temperature, surface area, distance.
  • Osmosis: Diffusion of water across semi-permeable membrane. Isotonic: No net movement. Hypotonic: Water enters. Hypertonic: Water leaves.
  • Facilitated Diffusion: Uses carrier/channel proteins for polar/large molecules.

Active Transport

  • Requires ATP: Movement against concentration gradient.
  • Examples: Sodium-potassium pump, glucose absorption in intestines.
  • Endocytosis: Bulk uptake (phagocytosis/pinocytosis). Exocytosis: Bulk release.

II. Genetics & Evolution

1. DNA Structure & Replication

DNA Structure

Nucleotide =
Phosphate + Deoxyribose Sugar + Nitrogenous Base
  • Double Helix: Two strands wound around each other
  • Base Pairing: A pairs with T (2 H-bonds), C pairs with G (3 H-bonds)
  • Antiparallel: Strands run in opposite directions (5' to 3' and 3' to 5')
  • Location: Nucleus (chromosomes), mitochondria, chloroplasts

Semi-Conservative Replication

  1. Unwinding: Helicase breaks H-bonds, separating the two strands
  2. 3' to 5': DNA polymerase adds nucleotides (leading strand, continuous)
  3. 5' to 3': Requires Okazaki fragments (lagging strand, discontinuous)
  4. Result: Two identical DNA molecules, each with one original strand

2. Protein Synthesis & Genetic Code

Transcription

  • Location: Nucleus
  • Template: DNA (coding strand)
  • Enzyme: RNA polymerase unzips DNA, builds mRNA
  • Product: Single-stranded mRNA (U replaces T)
  • Processing: 5' cap added, 3' poly-A tail added, introns removed

Translation

  • Location: Ribosomes (rough ER or free in cytoplasm)
  • Initiation: Start codon (AUG) signals ribosome to begin
  • tRNA: Anticodons match mRNA codons, bring amino acids
  • Elongation: Peptide bonds link amino acids (dipeptides → polypeptide)
  • Termination: Stop codon releases polypeptide chain

Genetic Code Key Features

Triplet Code: Each codon (3 nucleotides) codes for 1 amino acid
Degenerate: Multiple codons may code for same amino acid (64 codons, 20 amino acids)
Universal: Same code used across all organisms (with rare exceptions)
Overlapping: Codons cannot overlap; read in correct reading frame

3. Inheritance Patterns

Dominant & Recessive

  • Homozygous Dominant (AA): Both alleles are dominant = Dominant phenotype
  • Heterozygous (Aa): One dominant, one recessive = Dominant phenotype
  • Homozygous Recessive (aa): Both alleles recessive = Recessive phenotype

Key Crosses

  • AA × aa: All Aa offspring (100% dominant phenotype)
  • Aa × Aa: 1AA : 2Aa : 1aa (3:1 ratio)
  • Aa × aa: 1Aa : 1aa (1:1 test cross ratio)

4. Evolution & Natural Selection

Evidence for Evolution

  • Fossil Record: Shows progression of species over time, transitional forms (e.g., archaeopteryx - bird-reptile)
  • Comparative Anatomy: Analogous structures (similar function - bat wing/insect wing), Homologous structures (similar origin - human arm, whale flipper, bird wing)
  • Comparative Embryology: Similar embryonic development suggests common ancestry
  • Molecular Biology: Similar DNA/protein sequences in different species; more similar = closer evolutionary relationship

Natural Selection Process

  1. Variation: Genetic diversity within population
  2. Competition: Limited resources cause "survival of the fittest"
  3. Adaptation: Beneficial traits become more frequent (gene frequency increases)
  4. Speciation: Accumulated changes lead to new species formation over time

III. Organisms & Env

1. Photosynthesis in Detail

Light-Dependent Reactions

  • Location: Thylakoid membranes in granum
  • Light Energy: Absorbed by chlorophyll in Photosystem II
  • Photolysis: H₂O → 2H⁺ + ½O₂ + 2e⁻ (oxygen released)
  • Products: ATP (energy), NADPH (reducing power), O₂
  • Summary: Light energy → Chemical energy

Light-Independent Reactions (Calvin Cycle)

  • Location: Stroma of chloroplast
  • CO₂ Fixation: CO₂ combines with RuBP (5-carbon) → 3-PG (3-carbon) via enzyme RuBisCO
  • Reduction: 3-PG reduced to G3P using ATP & NADPH from light reactions
  • Regeneration: Some G3P regenerates RuBP to continue cycle
  • Product: Net glucose synthesis (6CO₂ → 1 glucose)

Overall Equation

6CO₂ + 6H₂O + Light Energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

2. Transpiration & Translocation

Transpiration

  • Definition: Loss of water vapor from plant leaves/stems
  • Route: Root uptake → Xylem → Leaves → Stomata → Atmosphere
  • INCREASES: High temperature, low humidity, high wind speed, high light intensity
  • DECREASES: Low temperature, high humidity, still air, darkness, closed stomata
  • Function: Cools plant, maintains water uptake, transport of minerals

Translocation

  • Definition: Transport of sugars from sources to sinks via phloem
  • Source: Leaves (photosynthesis) and storage organs
  • Sink: Growing tissues, roots, flowers, fruits (storage/growth)
  • Mechanism: Active transport loads sugars at source, creates osmotic gradient, drives flow to sink
  • Vessels: Sieve tube elements with companion cells in phloem

3. Nutrient Cycles & Ecology

Carbon Cycle

  • Photosynthesis: Plants convert CO₂ to glucose
  • Respiration: Organisms release CO₂ back to atmosphere
  • Decomposition: Bacteria/fungi break down dead matter → CO₂
  • Combustion: Fossil fuels burn → CO₂ released
  • Sinks: Oceans absorb CO₂, sedimentary rocks (fossil fuels) store carbon

Energy Flow in Ecosystems

  • Trophic Levels: Producers (plants) → Consumers (herbivores, carnivores, omnivores) → Decomposers
  • Energy Transfer: Only ~10% of energy passes to next trophic level
  • Food Chains: Linear path (grass → rabbit → fox)
  • Food Webs: Complex interconnected food chains
  • Pyramid of Numbers: Usually decreases up trophic levels (exceptions: oak tree/parasites)

4. The Nitrogen Cycle

Bacteria Type Process / Function
Nitrogen-fixing Converts N₂ gas into ammonium/nitrates. Brings N into food chain.
Nitrifying Converts ammonium → nitrites → Nitrates.
Denitrifying Converts nitrates back into N₂ gas. Depletes soil fertility.

IV. Health & Diseases

1. Pathogens & Transmission

Pathogen Type Examples Structure / Features Transmission Routes
Bacteria E. coli, TB bacillus, cholera Prokaryotic, cell wall, flagella, extracellular toxins Droplets, contaminated food/water, wounds
Viruses HIV, influenza, COVID-19, measles Nucleic acid core + protein coat (capsid), oblige intracellular parasites Respiratory droplets, blood, sexual contact, insect vectors
Fungi Candida (thrush), Aspergillus, ringworm Eukaryotic, chitin cell wall, spores, dimorphic species Inhalation of spores, skin contact, opportunistic infection
Protozoa Plasmodium (malaria), Giardia, trypanosomes Eukaryotic, motile (cilia/flagella), some with life cycles Insect vectors (mosquitoes, ticks), contaminated water

2. Defense Mechanisms - Three Lines

1st Line: Barriers

  • Physical: Skin (keratinized barrier), mucous membranes, cilia
  • Chemical: Lysozyme (tears, saliva), gastric acid (kills pathogens), sebum (antimicrobial)

2nd Line: Innate Immunity

  • Phagocytosis: WBCs (neutrophils, macrophages) engulf & digest
  • Inflammation: Histamine → vasodilation, increased permeability, redness/swelling/heat
  • Complement: Proteins cascade, mark pathogens for destruction

3rd Line: Specific Immunity

  • B Cells: Recognize antigen → plasma cells produce antibodies (humoral immunity)
  • T Cells: Cytotoxic T cells kill infected cells (cell-mediated immunity)
  • Memory: Long-lived B/T cells remember antigens

3. Immunological Memory & Vaccination

Primary vs Secondary Response

Primary: First exposure, long latent period (days), low antibody peak (IgM), fades quickly
Secondary: Re-exposure, SHORT latent period (hours), HIGH antibody peak (IgG), LONG-lasting immunity, faster response

Vaccination Strategy

  • Live attenuated: Weakened pathogen (e.g., MMR) - strong immunity
  • Inactivated: Dead pathogen (e.g., flu) - safer, needs boosters
  • Subunit: Isolated antigen (e.g., COVID mRNA/protein subunits)
  • Goal: Prime immune system without causing disease
Secondary Response Graph: Must show "shorter latent period", "steeper rise in antibody production", "higher peak concentration", and "sustained elevated levels" compared to primary response. IgG dominates instead of IgM.

V. Physiology

1. Nervous vs Endocrine Systems

Feature Nervous System Endocrine System
Message Type Electrical impulses (action potentials) Chemical hormones via bloodstream
Target Cells Specific neurons / effectors ANY cell with matching receptors
Speed Very Fast (milliseconds) Slow (seconds to minutes)
Duration Short-lived (immediate) Long-lasting (hours to days/weeks)
Best For Rapid responses (reflex arc, muscle contraction) Sustained changes (growth, reproduction, metabolism)

2. Kidney & Osmoregulation

Nephron Structure

  • Renal Corpuscle: Bowman's capsule + Glomerulus (blood capillary)
  • First Convoluted Tubule: Selective reabsorption of useful substances
  • Loop of Henle: Descending (permeable to water), Ascending (permeable to solutes)
  • Second Convoluted Tubule: Fine-tuning reabsorption
  • Collecting Duct: ADH-regulated water reabsorption

Three-Stage Filtration

  1. Ultrafiltration (Bowman's capsule): High BP forces small molecules (glucose, urea, water, ions) through, but NOT large proteins/blood cells
  2. Selective Reabsorption (Tubules): Active transport + passive transport reclaims glucose, ions, amino acids, SOME water
  3. Osmoregulation (Collecting Duct): ADH controls water reabsorption for final urine concentration

3. ADH Control Loop

Normal Blood Water Potential: Hypothalamus has osmoreceptors at set point
If Water Loss (e.g., Sweating): Blood becomes hypertonic → Osmoreceptors detect → Pituitary releases MORE ADH → Collecting duct becomes MORE permeable → MORE water reabsorption → Urine becomes concentrated & reduced in volume → Water potential restored
If Water Intake (e.g., Drinking 1L): Blood becomes hypotonic → Osmoreceptors detect → Pituitary releases LESS ADH → Collecting duct becomes LESS permeable → LESS water reabsorption → Urine becomes dilute & large in volume → Water potential restored

4. Reproduction & Hormonal Control

Female Menstrual Cycle (28 days)

  • Menstruation (Days 1-4): FSH ↓, Estrogen ↓ → Uterine lining breaks down
  • Follicular (Days 5-14): FSH ↑ → Ovarian follicles develop → Estrogen ↑ → Uterine lining thickens
  • Ovulation (Day 14): LH surge → Egg released from ovary
  • Luteal (Days 15-28): Progesterone ↑ (corpus luteum) → Uterium ready for implantation → If no fertilization, hormones ↓ → cycle repeats

Male Control (Continuous)

  • Hypothalamus: Releases GnRH (constant)
  • Anterior Pituitary: FSH → Spermatogenesis in seminiferous tubules
  • Testis: Produces Testosterone (Leydig cells) → Secondary sexual characteristics, maintains sperm production
  • Feedback: Testosterone inhibits GnRH/FSH if high (negative feedback loop)

VIII. Biotechnology

1. Gene Cloning & Recombinant DNA

Steps in Gene Cloning

  1. Isolate: Extract target gene from source DNA
  2. Cut: Use SAME restriction enzyme on target DNA AND plasmid vector → Sticky ends (cohesive ends)
  3. Ligate: DNA ligase seals phosphodiester bonds → Recombinant plasmid formed
  4. Transform: Insert into host cell (usually bacteria like E. coli)
  5. Identify: Select clones with desired gene (antibiotic resistance markers)

Applications of Gene Technology

  • Medicine: Insulin production (diabetics), growth hormone, blood clotting factors
  • Pharming: Transgenic animals/plants produce therapeutic proteins in milk/seeds
  • Agriculture: Disease resistance, herbicide tolerance (Bt corn, herbicide-resistant soybeans)
  • Diagnostics: Genetic screening, pathogen detection
  • Gene Therapy: Replace defective genes (SCID, cystic fibrosis - in development)

2. DNA Fingerprinting

Method: RFLP Analysis

  1. Extract DNA: From blood, semen, hair, saliva (any cell with nucleus)
  2. Restriction Enzymes: Cut at recognition sites (VNTR regions - Variable Number Tandem Repeats)
  3. Gel Electrophoresis: Fragments separated by size (smaller = migrate faster/further)
  4. Southern Blot: Transfer to membrane, probe with radioactive DNA, create band pattern
  5. Compare: Each person has unique fingerprint (except identical twins)

Applications

  • Forensic Science: Match suspects to crime scenes (99.99% accuracy)
  • Paternity Testing: Establish biological relationships (child must match alleles from both parents)
  • Wildlife Conservation: Track genetic diversity in endangered populations
  • Animal Breeding: Verify breed authenticity, prevent inbreeding
  • Immigration: Identify family relationships for visa purposes
